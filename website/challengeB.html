Part Two:
To the Stars

Having successfully passed the comprehension exam, you are admitted into the Laser Room. You see a gigantic machine pointed toward the sky with a rather simple control panel of two large buttons. The label on the machine reads "P.I.L." which stands for "Pulsar Intercepting Laser".

On the monitor, you see a map of the pulsars within the laser's range.

[ click to view full sized image ]

You are briefed on the PIL by the lead technician:

  • The PIL will emit a frequency of light in the direction of your provided coordinates, which hits the pulsar, causing it to flood out in all directions until it reaches an edge.
  • This reaction, if present on the entire pulsar, will emit 200 gigajoules (GJ) of energy, received by the PIL's sensors.
  • Unfortunately, a single pulse of the PIL costs 100 GJ so the net energy gained from completely filling a pulsar is 100 GJ, not 200 GJ.
  • If a pulsar is completely filled more than once, the PIL's sensors are able to intercept 1100 GJ from it instead of 200 GJ.
    It does not matter if the pulsar is filled more than twice. It will only either emit 200 GJ for one fill or 1100 GJ for more than one.

She continues to explain how the PIL's sensors are able to receive and store the energy from the pulsars, but you start to zone out. Science has never been your strong suit. What you do know, however, is that your job is to determine if the energy gained from your printout of coordinates (your puzzle input) is worth the cost of running the PIL and, if so, how much energy is net.

You notice that the pulsars on the monitor overlap and manage to ask the lead technician before she leaves if the laser will flood to the edge of the pulsar you hit only, or to any edge of any pulsar it encounters when they overlap.
She lets you know that the PIL only floods until it encounters any edge including the boundaries of the map and to think of the map as a 2D grid of pixels. Each of your coordinates targets a pixel on the map. You can press the "scan" button as many times as you like to check your net energy, and the PIL will not waste any energy. Energy is only lost when retrieving the pulsars' energy by pushing the "collect" button which will only be done once you deliver your results to the lead technician.

For example, looking at a smaller map and list of coordinates:

66, 147,
120, 123,
329, 181,
368, 339 Coordinates are indicated by red dots on the following map

After the PIL floods those areas you see them indicated in yellow on the following map

There are two full pulsars colored (circled). The other yellow area does not count because the pulsar is not fully flooded.

Two full pulsars flooded only once provides 400 GJ of energy. But four coordinates cost 400 GJ of energy, making the net 0 GJ. This process does not seem as efficient as it could be. You open the metal cover on the side of the laser labeled "CALIBRATIONS" and inside you see an empty rectangular slot like you'd find in a camera. You open the desk drawer looking for something that would fit inside that slot and discover a small square mirror that is the perfect length for the slot. Of course! You could double the output of the laser by mirroring the x and y coordinates!

This would mean, at the energy cost of your four coordinates, (66, 147) (120, 123) (329, 181) (368, 339) you could fill eight coordinates worth of pulsars! (66, 147) (120, 123) (329, 181) (368, 339)
(147, 68) (123, 120) (181, 329) (339, 368)

Two coordinates in each area of the upper left pulsar painted cyan to indicate it was flooded twice. A single coordinate in the bottom, left-of-the-middle pulsar painted yellow to indicate it was flooded only once.

The other pulsars on the right side are not completely filled, either getting interrupted by the boundary, or missing a small area (circled in red).

The one complete cyan pulsar provides an energy gain of 1100 GJ and the one complete yellow pulsar provides 200 GJ. When you subtract the 400 GJ it cost to send a pulse to these eight coordinates with the mirror, you get a total net energy of 900 GJ.

Lucky for you, you are a wiz at Python and just so happen to know there is a PIL Library at your disposal. You can bang out your coordinates in no time!

HINT (mouse-over to view):

PIL Library (pillow) comes with a built-in flood fill function, but try to use the flood fill function you made for Part One instead. numpy.array() can easily take a PIL.Image object and turn it into an array of RGB values representing each pixel. You can also use the PIL.Image.fromarray() method to take a numpy array turn it back into an image to view your results.
